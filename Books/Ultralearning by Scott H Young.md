## The 9 Principles
1. Metalearning
	- Do proper background research before your start a project, make sure you are going the right way to the right place.
	- Longer term; Once you have some projects under your belt, you will understand your capacity, and strategies that worked in the past.
	- Answer; Why, What and How
		- Why?
			- Instrumental and Intrinsic 
			- If you know why, you can be more specific about the what and better plan how
			- Important to make sure your why leads to what, otherwise you could be wasting time on activity that will not lead to the actual result you want.
			- Instrumental; If in doubt, setup a in person meeting or digital call with someone who has what you want (software architect at big company?).
		- What?
			- On Paper, write everything you will lead to learn. Use 3 columns; Concepts, Facts and Procedures;
			- Concepts: Anything that needs to be understood (Math and physics).
			- Facts: Anything that needs to be memorized.
			- Procedures: Anything that needs to be practiced.
			- Look over this list and identify the difficult parts.
		- How?
			- Invest time collecting the best resources.
			- Understand what needs to be emphasized (most important / practical) / less emphasized (least important).
	- Try to keep metalearning activity to 10% of overall project to make sure you are doing enough not wasting time and to prevent you doing too much and procrastinating.
2. Focus
	- Problem 1; Procrastination
		- Practice identification of Procrastination; Are you doing it to avoid work you find painful / boring? Or is something else more pleasurable taking you away?
		- Just start, once you start, the feeling of discomfort will often go away; start a 5 minute timer > graduate to a 25 pommodorro > 50 mins > longer blocks in the calendar.
		- Dont feel bad to start from the beginning if you get stuck are struggling.
	- Problem 2; Getting distracted
		- Ignore flow; If your are learning at the edge of yoru ability (which you should be), you will not experience flow.
			- Therefore do not be scared of the 25 min pommoddoro.
		- "50 minutes to an hour is a good length of time for many learning tasks"
		- If you cannot get enough of these to fit your goal, and need longer sessions, then make sure to practice interleaving.
		- Sources
			- Environments; Is our phone off? Is music off? Multitasking is impossible, so setup your environment (both digital and other) to support this reality.
			- Task; Just reading / watching a video can be easy to zone out, more engaging activity like active recall, making something or writing and explaining ideas aloud, far more engaging.
			- Mind; Re-occurring negative thoughts can hinder focus. Important to continue the learning practice anyway as this strengthens your ability to pay attention in the future.
3. Directness
	- The idea of learning being closely tied with the real application you want to apply the skill.
		- Example; You want better employment opportunities, don't sit random certificates or trainings, look at the exact skills / software are using and train them specifically.
	- Easiest way to learn, is doing the thing you want to learn!
	- Can resolve the issues with "Transfer" (see book) by learning in the context you want to apply the skill, instead of trying to apply what you have learned afterwards
	- Tactics;
		- Project Based Learning
			- **Write a project from scratch unassisted**
		- Immersive Learning
			- **Contribute to an open source project.**
		- The Flight Simulator Method
		- The Overkill Approach
4. Drill
	- *"Which aspect of the skill, if you improved it, would cause the greatest improvement to your abilities overall for the least amount of effort"* 
	- The Direct-Then-Drill Approach; Try applying what you are learning directly, and identify the areas you are still having trouble or lacking confidence. Rate-determining step. 
	- Develop drills and practices the smallest part of that area and hammer away.
	- Come back to direct practice with the drilled skilled 
	- Repeat.
	- Okay to be wrong about the limiting step. Try it out, and then use feedback to find the actual limiter.
	- Strategies
		- Drill 1: Time Slicing
			- Take small slice of song for example and do it over and over.
		- Drill 2: Cognitive Components
			- Learning language, can isolate grammar, pronunciation, and vocabulary can be isolated
		- Drill 3: The Copycat
			- For creative works, copy other. For drawing, tracing focus on the technical aspect without the decisions around placement of objects. 
		- Drill 4: The Magnifying Glass Method
			- Spend more time on purpose doing the part of the skill you want to drill. 
		- Drill 5: Prerequisite Chaining
			- Start too hard, and go back to the prerequsites when you need them; *"Eric Barone, for instance, started his pixel art experiments simply by making them. When he struggled with certain aspects, such as colors, he went back, learned color theory, and repeated his work"*.
5. Retrieval
	- Active recall is by far the best way to increase memory retention!
	- Creating an optimal gap between learning and recall practice is most effective.
	- Test yourself before your even start and then again as you go.
	- How to practice:
		- Tactic 1: Flash Cards
		- Tactic 2: Free Recall
			- "A simple tactic for applying retrieval is, after reading a section from a book or sitting through a lecture, to try to write down everything you can remember on a blank piece of paper"
		- Tactic 3: The Question-Book Method
			- Rephrase notes into questions to be answered later.
			- Limit yourself to few key questions per questions to make sure you are summarizing and not just filling the most basic facts.
		- Tactic 4: Self-Generated Challenges
			- Create a list of challenges you can do to put the theory into practice.
		- Tactic 5: Closed-Book Learning
			- Bar your self from reading / searching for the answer, generate notes without reading.
6. Feedback
	- The right kinds of feedback can help you improve your practice.
	- If your gathering public feedback, have a strategy for sorting the noise through to the signal. What is based on what you are actually doing / have control over?
	- Use feedback from tests to assess your learning and speed.
		- If feedback is every too positive, takes harder tests to find the ideal challenge.
	- Encourage the gathering of public feedback
		- **Contribute to public repos, invite feedback, release your product into the wild.**
7. Retention
	- How to remember what your learn?
		- Spacing: Repeat to Remember
			- No cramming - spread learning
			- Plan into refresh projects.
			- Increase spacing timeouts between problem.
		- Proceduralization: Automatic Will Endure
			- Make something your learning an automated procedure.
				- ***"You may aim to completely proceduralize the process you use to start working on a new programming project, for example, so that you can get over that hump in the process of writing a new program. "***
		- Overlearning: Practice Beyond Perfect
			- Repeated practice after the point of success keeps knowledge for longer. 
			- Can continue practicing the core elements of the skill
			- OR make start on the learning the next level of knowledge; Algebra > calculus.
		- Mnemonics: A Picture Retains a Thousand Words
			- Employ the visual-memory system for long term retention.
			- 
8. Intuition
	- *"Dig deep before building up"*
	- How to build your intuition:
		- Don't give up on solving problems yourself too easily - set a timer for 10 mins at the point you feel like looking for the answer to push yourself.
		- Don't take the questions of how anything works at face value. Always ask questions and attempt to dig deeper to really understand.
		- Develop concrete instances of the problem in context. If you cannot, then you might have more to learn
	- **Feynman Technique**
		- Steps
			- **Write a concept or problem on a piece of paper as the header.**
			- **Below, write a detailed description of the problem as you were teaching someone else.**
			- **If you cannot or get stuck, review the material to find the answer.**
		- Applications
			- When you are struggling to understand something.
			- When you cannot solve a problem or understand the technique.
			- For core concepts underpinning what you are trying to learn.
9. Experimentation
	- True mastery is built through the creation of original ideas / artifacts, not just regurgitation.
	- Experiment with your methods of learning, types of materials to find what suits you best.
	- How to...
		- Tactic 1: Copy, Then Create. Copy creative work from the existing experts to experiment with technique and attempt to find the exceptional elements.
		- Tactic 2: Compare Methods Side-by-Side. If you have a new learning technique you want to try out, try it alongside another one at the same time so you can quantify the results.
		- Tactic 3: Introduce New Constraints. Add limitations to force yourself to developer new capabilities.
		- Tactic 4: Find Your Superpower in the Hybrid of Unrelated Skills, Use your programming skills to help with your next learning project etc..tristan.ke
		- Tactic 5: Explore the Extremes. *"Pushing out to an extreme in some aspect of the skill you’re cultivating, even if you eventually decide to pull it back to something more moderate, is often a good exploration strategy. This allows you to search the space of possibilities more effectively, while also giving you a broader range of experience."*
## Your First Ultralearning Project
1. Do your research (metalearning)
	- 1. *What are you going to learn and what is the scope?*
		- A SMART goal encompassing your desired learning outcome.
		- "Learn enough cloud computing to pass the Azure Developer Associate exam", instead of "Learn cloud computing".
	- 2. What is the primary resource your are going to use?
		- Books, videos, classes, tutorials, guides, past exams, tests, mentors, coaches,  peers.
			- Have these downloaded, bought and organized before your start.
		- It's your starting point: “I’m going to read and complete the exercises in a book on Python programming for beginners”
	- 3. How have others learned in the past?
		- Search online for learners. Note impactful techniques, resources and strategies.
	- 4. How would you practice the skill?
		- How is this skill used in outside of learning?
		- Learning about cloud computing? Try to deploy a service, implementing scaling etc...
	- 5. Additional drills and backup materials.
		- If you feel like you don't have enough by covering your main material, have some potential backup material drill.
2. Schedule your time.
	- How much time will you commit per week?
	- When will you practice / study?
		- Time block it in advance. Lunch breaks are a good free hour. Weekends morning can be productive also.
	- What is the time line for your outcome?
		- Estimate how much you have committed and how much time you need.
		- Aim for 1-3 months MAX. If longer, try to break down goal into smaller increments.
			- Track progress towards outcome.
	- Take all of this information and put it in your calendar / print a physical timeline reminder.
	- Consider a pilot week. Do a strict trial run of your plan for a week, and see how you feel. Can you go harder or is it already too ambitious?
3. Execute the plan
	 - Regularly review your process and evaluate your alignment with the 9 principles:
		 - 1. METALEARNING. Have I done research into what are the typical ways of learning this subject or skill? Have I spent about 10 percent of the total time on preparing my project?
		 - 2. FOCUS. Am I focused when I spend time learning, or am I multitasking and distracted? Am I skipping learning sessions or procrastinating? When I start a session, how long does it take before I’m in a good flow? How long can I sustain that focus before my mind starts to wander? How sharp is my attention? Should it be more concentrated for intensity or more diffuse for creativity?
		 - 3. DIRECTNESS. Am I learning the skill in the way I’ll eventually be using it? If not, what mental processes are missing from my practice that exist in the real environment? 
		 - 4. DRILL. Am I spending time focusing on the weakest points of my performance? What is the rate-limiting step that is holding me back? Does it feel as though my learning is slowing down and that there’s too many components of the skill to master? If so, how can I split apart a complex skill to work on smaller, more manageable components of it?
		 - 5. RETRIEVAL. Am I spending most of my time reading and reviewing, or am I solving problems and recalling things from memory without looking at my notes? Do I have some way of testing myself, or do I just assume I’ll remember? Can I successfully explain what I learned yesterday, last week, a year ago? How do I know if I can?
		 - 6. FEEDBACK. Am I getting honest feedback about my performance early on, or am I trying to dodge the punches and avoid criticism? Do I know what I’m learning well and what I’m not? Am I using feedback correctly, or am I overreacting to noisy data?
		 - 7. RETENTION. Do I have a plan in place to remember what I’m learning long term? Am I spacing my exposure to information so it will stick longer? Am I turning factual knowledge into procedures that I’ll retain? Am I overlearning the most critical aspects of the skill?
		 - 8. INTUITION. Do I deeply understand the things I’m learning, or am I just memorizing? Could I teach the ideas and procedures I’m studying to someone else? Is it clear to me why what I’m learning is true, or does it all seem arbitrary and unrelated?
		 - 9. EXPERIMENTATION. Am I getting stuck with my current resources and techniques? Do I need to branch out and try new approaches to reach my goal? How can I go beyond mastering the basics and create a unique style to solve problems creatively and do things others haven’t explored before?
4. Review Your Results
	- How did your project go?
	- Were you able to stick to the plan?
	- Did you have to make a change and how did that help?
	- What changes did you make that you would want to included in your next project?
5. Choose to maintain you skill
	- Entropy is inevitable, what is your plan to combat?
	- You could designate weekly  -> monthly practice going forward.
	- Or you can pick a new project in a similar domain, moving towards mastery.
