# Process
3 Step Process For Studying Material
1. Priming
	1. Start your map before.
	2. Skim through headers, note 3-5 main topics.
	3. Attempt any questions or problem now (see "Problem First Approach" [THIS ASIAN Cramming Method Made Me Study Dangerously Fast (I re-built their system from scratch)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FsPYs6drGyY&ab_channel=ZainAsif))
2. "Notes"
	1. Read through the material again, taking only keyword notes, things you could realistically include on a mind map.
		- *TIP: Especially words you don't know the meaning of or came up in the questions.*
	2. Write down any additional questions and answer from the material or your own mind. 
3. Mindmapping
	1. Close your notes, and try to draw the mind map again. Focus on the relationships, use visuals.
	2. After your done, review your notes and add any important details you missed.
	3. Import the most relevant questions to Anki.
	4. Plan a future session to do the mind map from memory, then compare with your finished one, and review / revise the errors where your got it wrong

2 Step Review Process
1. Mix in a bit of both
	1. Testing, create and sit mock exams for previous materials
	2. MindMap from memory.
2. Only read your notes / the material again, for questions you got wrong or MindMap sections you missed, can save time not reviewing the rest.

# Core Principles

## Encoding
1. Chunking
	- **Priming.** Prime your mind and build the mental scaffolding, by reading section headers and chapter questions beforehand.
	- **Mindmap or Spider Diagram**. Build your relationships of topics and subjects as you learn.
2. Focus
	- **Pomodoro**. Set your intentions and do a 25 min interval of focus work, to help get over the "starting" hurdle. Helps you focus on the process, not the product.
	- **No Multitasking**. Avoid music, distracting notifications 
3. Memorization
	- **Use visualization and storytelling**. For hard to remember things, use Mind Palace visualization and creative storytelling to be able to recall.
## Retrieval
1. Active Recall
	- **Don't take exhaustive notes.** Read a few pages, then summarize, identify some keywords.
	- **Take mock exams.** Active learning beats passive learning.
2. Spaced Repetition
	-  **Use Anki.** After finishing a chapter, lesson or lecture think of some cards to create. Quick review your material for specifics.
3. Interleaving
	- . **Work on different things.** Break up large chunks of work with different types of problems.
## DONT
1. . Illusions
	- **Re-reading as learning.** Is time consuming and not as effective as active forms of learning (testing, mock exams, Anki, mindmaps).
	- **Highlighting or too many notes**. Give the illusion because you are doing something BUT is not helping you keep the knowledge in your brain. Exams, interviews and on the spot problem solving are about what is in the brain, not on a a pieces of paper or in obsidian.
DO
2. **Problem first approach.** As part of priming for a new topic, try the problems first. Don't look at the answers, ask yourself what confuses you and what you ae supposed to know to solve this problem.
3. **Microtesting**. Always looks to give yourself small test and exams, practices active recall and if you space it out correctly, space repetition.
	1. Week Subject Rapid Testing; Pick the weakest topic. Do 10-20 exam questions without notes. Do aggressive error analysis, 2x the time you spend trying to solve the problem (improved metacognition). Self teach the mistake.

## [The Ultimate Guide to the PERFECT Mindmap (6-Step Checklist)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzbQ8qZKpeg&ab_channel=ArcherNewton)
- Why mindmap?
	- The artifact, the map itself, does not really matter.
	- Instead, following the process or creating it, help solidify the knowledge IN OUR BRAINS
	- "But if feels too hard and is tiring"
		- Because, you are actually learning and creating knowledge.
			- Replacing the illusion, with deep learning.
1. Mindset
	- Always remind yourself "A mind map is for outlining the BIG concepts, not for remembering every little details"
		- Details are what cards | spaced repetition is for.
	- [ ] *"My mind map represent the main concepts rather than small details"*
2. Intention
	- What are you trying to learn?
		- Be intentional
			- Example: Ice Cream, but is it how to sell ice cream or the history of ice cream?
		- [ ] *"I have determined broad questions that my mind map should answer, based on the learning outcomes and my intuition"*
3. Words
	- List of keywords on the topic.
		- Important OR you don't know the meaning of.
		- *"Look for the nouns"*
		- Look ahead
			- Review learning objectives.
		- Take no more that 5 mins for this stage
	- [ ] *"I have prepared a lit of keywords based on my resources."
4. Chunking
	- Group your keywords
		- At the abstraction level required, to address your intention.
	- Good chunking?
		- [ ] *"How do these keywords relate to each other?"*
		- [ ] *"What are their similarities and differences?"*
		- [ ] *"What's the best way to represent how they relate to the overall purpose of how I intend to use this information?"*
5. Mapping
	- The previous steps gives us chunking
	- Ask questions about our chunks we asked about our keywords
		- [ ] *"How do these chunks relate to each other?"*
		- [ ] *"What are their similarities and differences?"*
		- [ ] *"What's the best way to represent how they relate to the overall purpose of how I intend to use this information?"
	- This should be difficult!
		- This is learning!
 6. Expanding
	 - Go through our other keywords, use them to EXPAND our mind map. 
		 - Get other keywords from readings, learning material, lectures etc.
	- [ ] *"Continue to add branches to your mind map based on your further learning from your resources e.g. lecture slides, textbook"*
	- [ ] *"Expand on these branches by adding keywords based on where it logically fits best!"*
