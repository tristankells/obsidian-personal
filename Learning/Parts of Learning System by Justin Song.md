# 📚 Learning System Guide

## **1. Priming**

**Description:**  
Priming is the process of forming a big-picture understanding before diving into details. It helps your brain create connections and reduces cognitive overload.

**Benefits:**

- Reduces overwhelm on new and complex concepts
- Increases learning speed

**Questions to Ask Yourself:**

- Do you gain a big-picture understanding of a new topic before learning the details?
- Do you briefly look through the topic before classes or study sessions?
- Do you think about how main ideas relate to each other before learning them deeply?
- Do you map out a basic understanding as a scaffold for deeper learning?
- Do you look for how ideas, facts, and concepts influence each other?
- Do you constantly relate new information to a big-picture understanding?

---

## **2. Encoding**

**Description:**  
Encoding is converting new information into memory. It’s a complex process and forms the bulk of studying time.

**Benefits:**

- Improves short- and long-term memory
- Saves time by reducing repetition

**Questions to Ask Yourself:**

- Do you look for ways to make new information more relevant and intuitive?
- Do you actively organise information to reduce rote memorisation?
- Do you group and rearrange information for better structure?
- Do you create analogies for complex ideas?
- Do you pause regularly to organise and simplify new information?

---

## **3. Reference**

**Description:**  
Reference is about strategic note-taking for details that don’t fit elsewhere. It helps streamline your process and reduce unnecessary effort.

**Benefits:**

- Necessary for efficient revision
- Reduces anxiety over details

**Questions to Ask Yourself:**

- Do you use note-taking to visually organise thoughts and explore relationships?
- Do you store detailed factual information differently from conceptual information?
- Do you minimise linear notes for conceptual learning?
- Do you use non-linear note-taking (e.g., mind maps) for conceptual learning?
- Do you spend time writing notes that don’t help connect ideas or simplify information?

---

## **4. Retrieval & Interleaving**

**Description:**  
Retrieval is recalling knowledge from memory; interleaving is applying it in diverse contexts. These strengthen retention and adaptability.

**Benefits:**

- Improves test and assessment performance
- Strengthens memory and retention
- Deepens mastery of new knowledge
- Improves complex problem-solving
- Increases ability to answer unexpected questions

**Questions to Ask Yourself:**

- Do you apply knowledge from memory within the first week or month of learning?
- Do you test your knowledge to identify gaps?
- Do you fill gaps by approaching information from different perspectives?
- Do you recall and apply knowledge in multiple ways?
- Do you reinforce isolated facts differently (e.g., flashcards) than conceptual learning?
- Do you use repetitive recall techniques like quizzes or flashcards?
- Do you actively look for ways your knowledge may be tested beyond expected scope?

---

## **5. Overlearning**

**Description:**  
Overlearning means going beyond what’s necessary to reinforce knowledge and fluency. It’s most useful for high-stakes assessments.

**Benefits:**

- Increases recall speed
- Reinforces memory
- Improves confidence by finding knowledge gaps

**Questions to Ask Yourself:**

- Do you learn more than you think you need to strengthen core material?
- Do you use high-volume techniques (e.g., quizzes, flashcards) early in learning?
- Do you overlearn at the right stage (after encoding and retrieval are solid)?
