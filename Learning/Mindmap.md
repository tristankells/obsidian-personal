# Why mindmap?
- Re-reading, highlighting, passive listening are not efficient or particularly effective ways to learn.
- Mindmaps are a **active** learning method which...
	- Makes learning meaningful
		- Forces connections between new knowledge and existing knowledge, positively impacting the ability to encode and retain.
	- Makes learning memorable
		- Invokes the Visual Memory system and create a mnemonic (similar to the LOCI or Mind Palace technique), which is shown to greatly improve recall.
	- Use correctly they help internalization information (taking from "external database" to "internal database") by encouraging:
		- active recall (much better than passive reading at getting information into your head).
		- understanding (links to existing knowledge and put together the big picture)
		- visualize (they enlist the Visual Memory system for increased retention)
# How to
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EGdqAJnl0vk&ab_channel=DrAlexYoung
- Chunk, organize, collect and doodle.
- Start the MindMap from memory, then use your material to flesh it out.
	- This encourages active recall and creating linking to your existing knowledge, instead of just turning your notes on a topic into a visual form.
- To use them in review, test yourself by trying to recreate the mind maps from memory (active recall) and use questions to deepen your understanding.

[Watch This BEFORE Studying With MINDMAPS - Effective And Ineffective Methods - With Full Demo - YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7nFsE2qPjic&ab_channel=TomWatchman)
- Before your even look at a topic, draw a mindmap from memory...
- Then read the material using an active read and recall method.
- Thinking and process what you have just read.
- Draw another mindmap from memory.
	- Map out with increasing detail
	- Imagine how you would explain to someone (see Feynman technique)
	- Once your have exhausted your memory, feel free to check the material for details to further expand your mindmap.
- ADVANCED: Redraw from memory again, and see if your can re-organise the content even better (this whole process is support internalization with the three core concepts; **recall,  understanding and visualize**)
	- Check details
- Now STOP, and use spaced repetition to repeat the process, and identify shortcomings.
- ADVANCED: Throw these all away. A mindmap is NOT a good reference material. The map is not a useful artifact, but instead creating is it causing the learning to go on internally.
	- Not a storage system.
	- If you are not worried about keeping them, you can go faster and tidy up will not be worth it.
	- Signals to your brain you need to retain this because you have not reminder in the future.
	- Decreases the pressure and hesitancy your feel.
- Afterwards, apply that information through testing.
- Mindmaps before you study will can make you review faster, by identifying errors your are weak or already very strong in.
# Avoid
- Drawing a Mindmap with the textbook open - Mininmal processing, this is just using the Mindmap as traditional **notes** and won't help your engage in active recall.
	- Captures limited understanding and lacks context.

# Core Principles
- Practice active recall.
- Use creativity to increase visual memorability; highlighting, colour, pictures.
